HOST1: Here's something that might reshape how you think about transformers. What if they're not just pattern matching? What if they're literally performing Bayesian inference, and you can prove it geometrically?
HOST2: OK, "prove" is doing a lot of heavy lifting there. People have been hand-waving about transformers doing Bayesian stuff for a while. What's different here?
HOST1: What's different is that previous claims were essentially unfalsifiable. You'd look at a large language model doing in-context learning and say, well, it looks Bayesian. But you can't verify that because you don't know what the true Bayesian answer is on natural language data. There's no ground truth posterior to compare against. And the model might just be memorizing patterns that happen to look Bayesian.
HOST2: So how do you get around that?
HOST1: They built what they call "Bayesian wind tunnels." Think of it like aerospace engineering. You can't test an airplane in the full complexity of the atmosphere until you understand its behavior in a controlled wind tunnel. Same idea here. They designed controlled environments where two things are true simultaneously. First, the correct Bayesian posterior is known in closed form, so you have an exact answer key. Second, the hypothesis space is so enormous that memorization is mathematically impossible.
HOST2: What kinds of tasks are we talking about?
HOST1: Four tasks, each testing different aspects of reasoning. The first is bijection learning. Imagine you have a shuffled mapping between inputs and outputs. The model sees input-output pairs one at a time, and after each pair, it should narrow down which mapping it's seeing. The Bayesian answer at each step is a uniform distribution over the remaining possibilities. So if you've seen five pairs out of a possible twenty elements, the posterior entropy should drop by exactly one bit each time you see a new pair. It's a perfect staircase.
HOST2: And the transformer actually produces that staircase?
HOST1: Down to a thousandth of a bit. They measured the entropy of the model's predictions position by position and compared it to the exact Bayesian entropy. The match is extraordinary. And these are small transformers, not billion-parameter models.
HOST2: What about the other tasks?
HOST1: The second is Hidden Markov Model filtering. This is harder because you're not just eliminating hypotheses, you're tracking a hidden state that evolves over time. The model sees a sequence of noisy observations and needs to infer the hidden state at each step. This requires recursive Bayesian updating, the forward algorithm, essentially chaining together posterior updates as new evidence arrives.
HOST2: So this tests whether transformers can do sequential belief revision, not just static elimination.
HOST1: Exactly. And again, the transformers nail it. They match the Bayesian posterior to high precision. But here's where it gets architecturally interesting. They also tested the same tasks on MLPs, LSTMs, and Mamba, that selective state-space model. And the results are wildly different across architectures.
HOST2: Different how?
HOST1: They developed this really elegant framework. They decomposed Bayesian inference into three primitives. First, belief accumulation: integrating evidence into a running posterior over time. Second, belief transport: propagating your beliefs forward when the hidden state itself is changing, like in an HMM where the state transitions stochastically. Third, random-access binding: storing a bunch of associations and retrieving the right one by content, like looking up a phone number by name rather than by position in a list.
HOST2: So different architectures can do different subsets of these primitives?
HOST1: Exactly, and this is the key theoretical contribution. Transformers can do all three. They're what the authors call "primitive complete." Mamba can do accumulation and transport but struggles with random-access binding. LSTMs can only do accumulation of static sufficient statistics, meaning they work when the running summary has a fixed size, but they fail when the summary itself needs to evolve or when you need content-based retrieval. And MLPs fail at everything.
HOST2: Wait, Mamba failing at binding is interesting. That's the in-context retrieval thing?
HOST1: Right. They tested associative recall, where the model sees a bunch of key-value pairs and then needs to retrieve a value given a key. This requires content-based routing: you need to look at the query key, find which stored pair matches, and pull out the value. Transformers do this naturally through the attention mechanism, which is literally a content-addressable memory lookup. Mamba doesn't have that mechanism. It compresses everything into a fixed-size hidden state, so it loses the ability to route by content.
HOST2: OK so that explains the architectural separation. But you mentioned geometry. Where does the geometry come in?
HOST1: This is the beautiful part. They didn't just show that transformers match Bayesian posteriors. They looked inside and figured out how. The geometric mechanism has three components. First, the residual stream acts as the belief substrate. The model's internal representation at each position literally encodes the current posterior distribution over hypotheses.
HOST2: So the high-dimensional vector in the residual stream has a direct geometric correspondence to the probability distribution the model is tracking?
HOST1: Yes. Second, the feed-forward networks perform the posterior update. When new evidence arrives, the FFN layers transform the representation in a way that corresponds to applying Bayes' rule. Third, attention provides content-addressable routing. It's the mechanism that lets the model look back at previous observations and pull the relevant information for the current update.
HOST2: That's a really clean decomposition. Residual stream equals beliefs. FFNs equal updates. Attention equals routing.
HOST1: And they found specific geometric signatures. The key bases in the attention mechanism are orthogonal, meaning each attention head looks for a distinct type of information. The query and key projections progressively align during training, and there's a low-dimensional manifold in the value space that's parameterized by posterior entropy.
HOST2: What does that mean, parameterized by entropy?
HOST1: Think of it this way. As the model gets more certain about the correct hypothesis, the posterior entropy drops. And the values that attention reads out live on a curved surface in high-dimensional space, where your position on that surface tells you how uncertain the model is. Early in a sequence when uncertainty is high, the values are in one part of the manifold. As evidence accumulates and uncertainty drops, they smoothly move to another part.
HOST2: That's gorgeous. So the model's internal geometry literally mirrors the mathematical structure of Bayesian inference.
HOST1: And here's a really cool training dynamics finding. During training, this value manifold "unfurls." It starts compressed and then unfolds into its final shape. But the attention patterns, which head attends to which position, those stabilize early and stay fixed while the manifold changes shape. They call this "frame-precision dissociation." The routing structure locks in first, and then the content that flows through that routing gradually aligns with the Bayesian posterior.
HOST2: OK I have to play devil's advocate. These are small, controlled transformers on toy tasks. How much can we really extrapolate to actual language models with billions of parameters doing natural language?
HOST1: That's the honest limitation, and the authors acknowledge it. The wind tunnel approach sacrifices scale for verifiability. You can't do this analysis on GPT-4 because you don't know the true posterior for natural language tasks. But the argument is that understanding the mechanisms in controlled settings gives you tools to look for the same structures in larger models. And there's already a bunch of evidence from other mechanistic interpretability work that large language models do organize information linearly and geometrically in ways that are consistent with these findings.
HOST2: And they're framing this as paper one of a trilogy?
HOST1: Yes, this is part one of the "Bayesian Attention Trilogy." Paper two apparently shows that this geometric structure arises naturally from gradient dynamics under cross-entropy training, so it's not an accident, it's a consequence of the training objective. And paper three extends the analysis to partially observed settings that are closer to natural language.
HOST2: So the claim is: transformers don't just approximate Bayesian inference. They implement it geometrically, and this is the inevitable result of how we train them.
HOST1: That's the trajectory, yes. And if it holds up, it would explain a lot. It would explain why linear probes work so well for finding concepts inside transformers. It would explain why the refusal direction paper we just talked about found such clean one-dimensional structure. If the model is organizing its representations as geometric Bayesian posteriors, then important concepts naturally align with linear subspaces.
HOST2: This connects those two papers in a way I didn't expect. The refusal direction is basically a posterior over "is this request harmful," and it lives on a line because Bayesian posteriors for binary questions are one-dimensional.
HOST1: Exactly. And that's why this theoretical work matters even though it's on small models. It's building the mathematical foundation that explains why all these empirical interpretability techniques work. The geometry isn't an accident. It's how transformers do inference.
HOST2: Beautiful theory work. I'm very curious to see if the trilogy sticks the landing when they get to natural language.
HOST1: Same. But even as a standalone result, proving that transformers can exactly implement Bayesian inference and showing the geometric mechanism by which they do it, that's a significant contribution to our understanding of what these models actually are.
