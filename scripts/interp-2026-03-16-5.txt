HOST1: We're going back to 2022 today. Back to the paper that started everything. If you've spent any time in mechanistic interpretability, you've heard of "Toy Models of Superposition" by Nelson Elhage, Chris Olah, and a huge team at Anthropic. But I want to actually walk through what this paper showed, because it's one of those foundational results that everyone cites but fewer people have really sat with.
HOST2: And it's especially relevant today because the first paper we covered this week, the BOWS paper on correlations and superposition, directly extends this work. So if you want to understand where the field is going, you need to understand where it started.
HOST1: Exactly. So let's start with the problem. If you look at individual neurons in a neural network, something weird happens. A single neuron might activate for cats, cars, and the concept of uncertainty. Three completely unrelated things. This phenomenon is called polysemanticity, and it's been the bane of interpretability research for years. Because if one neuron means many things, you can't just read off what the network is thinking by looking at which neurons are active.
HOST2: And the question nobody could answer was: why? Why do networks do this? Is it a bug? Is it a feature? Is it an artifact of training?
HOST1: That's exactly what Elhage and Olah set out to explain. And their answer is superposition. The key insight is that the world has more concepts than any neural network has dimensions. If you think about everything a language model needs to represent, from individual words to abstract concepts to factual knowledge, there might be millions or billions of relevant features. But a typical hidden layer has maybe a few thousand dimensions.
HOST2: So the model has to pack more features than it has room for.
HOST1: Right. And the question is how. Their answer: the model arranges feature directions so that they overlap in the high-dimensional space. Each feature gets a direction, but those directions aren't orthogonal. They interfere with each other. This is superposition. And the reason it works is sparsity.
HOST2: Walk me through the sparsity argument. Why does sparsity make this possible?
HOST1: Think about it this way. Most features are active only a small fraction of the time. The "cat" feature fires when there's a cat in the input, which is rare in the grand scheme of all possible inputs. The "car" feature fires when there's a car. If "cat" and "car" features point in slightly overlapping directions, they'll interfere with each other. But if only one of them is active at any given time, the interference doesn't actually matter. The model can recover the active feature because the other one is off.
HOST2: So the model is making a bet. It's saying: these features almost never co-occur, so I can store them in overlapping directions and just deal with the occasional collision.
HOST1: Exactly. And Elhage and Olah studied this with the simplest possible model. A ReLU autoencoder where the input has more dimensions than the bottleneck layer. Say you have a hundred features but only twenty dimensions. Can the model still represent all hundred features? And the answer is: yes, if the features are sparse enough.
HOST2: How does the geometry work?
HOST1: This is where it gets beautiful. They found that the model arranges feature directions according to the geometry of uniform polytopes. Think of regular geometric shapes in high dimensions. In two dimensions, you know how you can arrange vectors to be as spread out as possible? Three vectors at 120 degrees, like a triangle. Four vectors as a square, or even better, two pairs of opposite vectors.
HOST2: The antipodal pair thing.
HOST1: Right. In higher dimensions, the optimal arrangements correspond to specific polytopes. The model is essentially solving a packing problem: how to arrange as many directions as possible in a limited-dimensional space so that pairwise interference is minimized. And they showed there's a clean phase transition.
HOST2: Phase transition in what sense?
HOST1: In the tradeoff between sparsity and importance. Each feature has two properties: how important it is for the model's loss, and how sparse it is, meaning how rarely it's active. If a feature is important but not sparse, the model dedicates a full dimension to it. No superposition needed. If a feature is important and very sparse, the model can pack it into superposition because collisions are rare. And there's a sharp boundary between these regimes. As you increase sparsity, features suddenly jump from "dedicated dimension" to "superposition." It's not a gradual transition. It's a phase change.
HOST2: That's really clean. And I remember they made a connection to adversarial examples?
HOST1: Yes, and this is one of the more provocative implications. If features are stored in superposition with overlapping directions, then small perturbations to the input can activate features that shouldn't be there. Think about it: if the "cat" and "car" directions overlap slightly, a tiny nudge to a "cat" input in the direction of "car" could activate the "car" feature. That's essentially what an adversarial example does. The connection isn't proven definitively in the paper, but they lay out compelling evidence that superposition creates a natural vulnerability to adversarial attacks.
HOST2: That's a much more satisfying explanation than "adversarial examples are mysterious bugs." If the model is fundamentally packing features into overlapping spaces, adversarial vulnerability is a structural consequence, not a random failure mode.
HOST1: Exactly. Now, the other huge contribution of this paper is the connection to dictionary learning and sparse autoencoders. If the model stores features in superposition, you should be able to recover those features by finding a sparse decomposition of the activations. You need a dictionary that's larger than the hidden dimension, because there are more features than dimensions, and you need sparsity because only a few features are active at once.
HOST2: And that's exactly what a sparse autoencoder does.
HOST1: Right. This paper didn't invent SAEs for interpretability. That came a bit later with the Bricken and Cunningham papers. But it provided the theoretical motivation. It explained why SAEs should work. If the true features are sparse and stored in superposition, then an overcomplete sparse dictionary should be able to recover them.
HOST2: Let me ask the critical question though. This is a toy model paper. Simple ReLU autoencoders with synthetic data. How much of this actually applies to real transformers?
HOST1: That's the million-dollar question, and honestly, the answer has evolved over the past four years. The toy model predictions about phase transitions and polytope geometry have been at least partially confirmed in real networks. People have found geometric structures in language model embeddings that match the polytope predictions. SAEs have been wildly successful at finding interpretable features, which validates the core superposition hypothesis. But there are also real gaps.
HOST2: Like the correlation thing from the BOWS paper we covered earlier.
HOST1: Exactly. The biggest limitation of this toy model is the assumption that features are uncorrelated. In the real world, features are deeply correlated. "December" and "Christmas" and "snow" and "holiday" all co-occur. The toy model treats interference as pure noise to be suppressed. But as we discussed earlier today, the BOWS paper shows that correlated features exploit interference constructively. So the toy model got the basic picture right, superposition is real and it's why neurons are polysemantic, but it missed a whole dimension of how superposition actually works in practice.
HOST2: It's like Newton's gravity. Correct enough to be incredibly useful, but incomplete in ways that matter for the real world.
HOST1: That's a great analogy. And just like general relativity needed Newton as a starting point, the BOWS paper and all the subsequent superposition research needed this paper as its foundation. You can't understand where the field is today without understanding what Elhage and Olah showed in 2022.
HOST2: What's the legacy? Four years out, what did this paper ultimately give us?
HOST1: Three things. First, the concept of superposition as a fundamental property of neural network representations. That's now standard vocabulary. Second, the theoretical motivation for sparse autoencoders as an interpretability tool. SAEs have become the dominant approach in mechanistic interpretability, and this paper is why. Third, a research program. The questions this paper raised, how does superposition interact with computation, what happens with correlated features, can we design architectures that don't superpose, those questions are still driving cutting-edge research today.
HOST2: And the BOWS paper we covered is literally extending the toy model framework to handle correlations. It's a direct intellectual descendant.
HOST1: Direct descendant. Same autoencoder setup, same analytical framework, just with realistic feature statistics instead of synthetic independent ones. And the result is a richer, more complete theory of superposition that explains phenomena the original couldn't.
HOST2: So if the original paper launched a thousand SAEs, the BOWS paper is recalibrating where those SAEs should aim.
HOST1: Well put. The original paper said: features are in superposition, go find them with sparse dictionaries. The BOWS paper adds: but some of those features are cooperating through their interference, so maybe your dictionaries need to account for that too.
HOST2: The field keeps building. Good stuff.
HOST1: It's a good time to be watching mechanistic interpretability.
