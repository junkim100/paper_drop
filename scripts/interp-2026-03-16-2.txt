HOST1: Here's a question that sounds simple but turns out to be really hard to answer. When a sparse autoencoder finds an interpretable feature in a language model, is that feature capturing actual meaning, or is it just picking up on surface-level patterns in how the text looks?
HOST2: That's the fundamental question for the whole SAE interpretability program, isn't it? If features are just fancy token detectors, then all those beautiful dashboards with labeled features are kind of misleading.
HOST1: Right. And the problem is, it's really hard to test. Because normally, if you change the words in a sentence, you change the meaning too. You can't easily hold meaning constant while varying the surface form. But a group led by Sripad Karne found a brilliant natural experiment to do exactly that. The paper is called "One Language, Two Scripts: Probing Script-Invariance in LLM Concept Representations," and it was presented at the UCRL Workshop at ICLR 2026.
HOST2: And the trick is Serbian, right? Because Serbian has this unique property.
HOST1: Serbian digraphia. Serbian is one of very few languages in the world that's actively written in two completely different scripts. Latin script and Cyrillic script. And this isn't like a historical curiosity. Serbian speakers use both scripts interchangeably in everyday life. There's a deterministic, lossless character mapping between them. You can convert any Serbian sentence from Latin to Cyrillic or back without losing a single bit of meaning.
HOST2: But from the tokenizer's perspective, these are completely different, right?
HOST1: Completely. When you feed the same Serbian sentence in Latin script versus Cyrillic script through the Gemma tokenizer, you get zero shared tokens. Not a single token in common. The character repertoires are just totally different. So from the model's input perspective, these look like they could be from entirely different languages.
HOST2: And yet the meaning is identical. That's a perfect controlled experiment.
HOST1: Exactly. So they built a dataset of thirty sentence triplets. Each triplet has an original sentence, a semantically equivalent paraphrase with different word choices, and an unrelated random sentence. Each of these exists in English, Serbian Latin, and Serbian Cyrillic. Two hundred seventy unique sentences total. Then they ran all of these through the Gemma model family, from the tiny 270 million parameter version all the way up to the 27 billion parameter version.
HOST2: And they used the Gemma Scope 2 SAEs, the ones Google DeepMind released?
HOST1: Right. JumpReLU sparse autoencoders with sixty-five thousand features, trained on model activations at multiple layers. For each sentence, they extract the active feature set at the last token position. Then they measure how much those feature sets overlap between different pairs of sentences using Jaccard similarity. Which is just the intersection divided by the union of the active feature sets.
HOST2: So what do they find?
HOST1: The headline result: identical sentences across scripts, zero shared tokens remember, activate highly overlapping SAE features. The average Jaccard similarity for cross-script identical sentences is about 0.58. And here's the kicker. That cross-script similarity is actually higher than the within-script paraphrase similarity.
HOST2: Wait, say that again. Changing the entire alphabet produces less divergence in SAE features than just rephrasing the same sentence within the same alphabet?
HOST1: That's exactly what they found. Think about what that means. The SAE features are more sensitive to word choice than to the writing system. If you change from "The cat sat on the mat" to "A feline rested on the rug" in Latin script, you get more feature divergence than if you convert "The cat sat on the mat" from Latin to Cyrillic, which changes every single token.
HOST2: That's a really strong result. The features genuinely care about meaning, not surface form.
HOST1: And they went further. They tested cross-script cross-paraphrase combinations. So original in Latin versus paraphrase in Cyrillic. These combinations are really unlikely to co-occur in training data. If you've changed both the script and the word choices, this is about as dissimilar as two versions of the same meaning can get at the surface level. And they still found substantial feature overlap, around 0.47 Jaccard. Way above the random baselines.
HOST2: That's important because it rules out memorization, right? The model isn't just matching patterns it saw during training.
HOST1: Exactly. The cross-language random baseline, comparing unrelated Serbian sentences to unrelated English sentences, sits around 0.19. The cross-script random baseline, unrelated Serbian sentences in different scripts, is around 0.28. And the meaningful comparisons are all far above those floors. There's a clean hierarchy: identical cross-script is highest, then cross-script paraphrase, then cross-script cross-paraphrase, then random. That's exactly what you'd predict if features capture a semantic hierarchy where meaning drives similarity.
HOST2: Does model scale matter?
HOST1: A lot. This is one of the cleanest scaling results I've seen. Cross-script similarity for identical sentences goes from about 0.50 at the 270 million parameter model up to 0.65 at 27 billion. Meanwhile, the random baselines go in the opposite direction, from about 0.42 down to 0.21 for cross-script random. So the gap between meaningful and random comparisons widens dramatically with scale.
HOST2: So bigger models are better at abstracting away from surface orthography. That makes intuitive sense. But is there any nuance there?
HOST1: There's an interesting subtlety. Cross-script cross-paraphrase similarity, where you change both script and words, stays roughly flat across scales. About 0.47 to 0.49 regardless of model size. The authors hypothesize two opposing effects canceling out. Larger models get better at recognizing equivalent content across scripts, which should push similarity up. But they also develop finer-grained features that distinguish subtle differences between paraphrases, which pushes it down. The net result is flat.
HOST2: Makes sense. Bigger models have higher resolution, so they see differences smaller models would miss. OK, let me push back a little. They only tested Serbian. How do we know this generalizes?
HOST1: That's the main limitation and the authors are upfront about it. Serbian digraphia is uniquely clean because the mapping is deterministic and lossless. Other multi-script situations are messier. Hindi and Urdu are linguistically very similar but use completely different scripts, Devanagari and Nastaliq, and the mapping isn't as clean. Chinese traditional and simplified characters don't have a one-to-one correspondence. So while Serbian is the perfect controlled experiment, extending this to other pairs is an open problem.
HOST2: And they only used Gemma with one SAE configuration, right?
HOST1: Right. Gemma family only, Gemma Scope 2 SAEs with sixty-five thousand features at medium sparsity. Different architectures, different SAE training recipes, or different sparsity levels could give different results. The dataset is also just thirty sentence triplets, which is small. It's enough for clear statistical trends, but a larger, more diverse corpus would strengthen the claims.
HOST2: Still, even with those caveats, this is a meaningful result. It tells us SAE features aren't just glorified n-gram detectors. They're capturing something genuinely semantic that persists even when you completely change the surface representation.
HOST1: And practically, I think this paper establishes Serbian digraphia as a reusable evaluation tool. If you're training a new SAE variant or a new model architecture, you can use this same paradigm to test whether your features are capturing meaning or surface patterns. That's really useful for the field.
HOST2: One more thing that caught my eye. The fact that cross-script random similarity is higher than cross-language random tells us something interesting. Even at the random baseline level, the model treats Latin and Cyrillic Serbian as more similar to each other than either is to English. Despite zero shared tokens.
HOST1: Right. The model has learned, somewhere in its representations, that these two scripts encode the same language. That shared-language knowledge bleeds through even into the random baseline. It's a nice piece of evidence that these models are building genuine linguistic structure, not just token statistics.
HOST2: Alright, this is a clean paper. Not the most technically complex work, but the experimental design is elegant and the implications are clear. SAE features care about meaning, not spelling.
HOST1: Well said. And for anyone worried that the interpretability tools we're building are just finding surface patterns, this is a reassuring data point.
