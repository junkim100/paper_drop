HOST1: Alright, so mechanistic interpretability has been an NLP and vision thing for a long time. Sparse autoencoders, circuits analysis, feature visualization, all of it developed on language models or image models. But there's this massive class of foundation models that nobody has tried to crack open yet. Time series models.
HOST2: And those are deployed in some pretty high-stakes places. Finance, energy grids, healthcare monitoring. You'd really want to know what they've actually learned.
HOST1: Exactly. So this paper, "Sparse Autoencoders Reveal Causal Feature Hierarchies in Time Series Foundation Models," is the first to apply SAEs to a time series foundation model. They picked Chronos-T5-Large, which is Amazon's time series model built on the T5 architecture, 710 million parameters. And what they found is genuinely surprising.
HOST2: What's the setup? How do you even apply SAEs to a time series model?
HOST1: It's more natural than you might think. Chronos-T5 works by taking continuous time series values, quantizing them into discrete tokens, four thousand ninety-six bins, and then processing them through a standard T5 encoder-decoder transformer. So under the hood, it's a regular transformer with a residual stream, attention layers, the works. They trained TopK sparse autoencoders on the residual stream activations at six different extraction points: three encoder layers covering early, middle, and final, two decoder layers, and the cross-attention output.
HOST2: And TopK SAEs are the ones where you keep only the top K active features at each step?
HOST1: Right. They set K to sixty-four, so at any given input, only sixty-four out of eight thousand one hundred ninety-two features are active. The expansion factor is eight times the hidden dimension. Pretty standard SAE recipe, just applied to a domain where nobody had done it before.
HOST2: So what did the features look like?
HOST1: They built a taxonomy by running the SAE features against synthetic diagnostic data. Time series with known properties: pure trends, seasonal patterns, level shifts, frequency sweeps, noisy signals. Then they classified each feature based on which diagnostic pattern it correlated with most. And the taxonomy reveals a beautiful depth hierarchy across the encoder.
HOST2: Walk me through it.
HOST1: Early encoder, block five. Very few features get labeled, only about five percent. The ones that do are dominated by high-frequency detection and volatility features. Think of it as the model doing raw signal processing. It's picking up on local wiggles and noise levels.
HOST2: Like the first convolutional layers in a vision model detecting edges.
HOST1: Great analogy. Then the mid-encoder, block eleven, which is where things get really interesting. About twenty-six percent of features get labeled here, and the dominant category is level shifts. Over a thousand features dedicated to detecting sudden changes, points where the time series jumps up or down abruptly. Plus a large cluster of noise-detection features. Seasonality is almost absent. The mid-encoder is essentially a change-detection hub.
HOST2: So it's not learning pretty patterns like "this has a weekly cycle." It's learning "something just changed dramatically here."
HOST1: Exactly. And then the final encoder, block twenty-three. This is the richest layer. Almost sixty percent of features get labels. Seasonality dominates with nearly fourteen hundred features. You also get a full spread of trend detection, frequency analysis, level shifts, everything. It's compressing a comprehensive temporal characterization that presumably gets fed to the decoder through cross-attention.
HOST2: OK, so there's a nice hierarchy. Low-level signal processing, then change detection, then a rich semantic summary. That's intuitive. But what about the causal experiments? How do you know these features actually matter for the model's predictions?
HOST1: This is the best part. They ran three hundred ninety-two single-feature ablation experiments. For each feature, they zero out that one feature's activation in the sparse code, decode back to get modified hidden states, patch those into the model's forward pass, and measure how much the forecast quality degrades. They used CRPS, continuous ranked probability score, which is the standard metric for probabilistic forecasts.
HOST2: And the result?
HOST1: Every single ablated feature produces a strictly positive CRPS degradation. One hundred percent of features are causally relevant. Not a single dud. That's a really strong validation that the SAE is finding genuine computational structure, not artifacts.
HOST2: Wow. OK, but are all features equally important?
HOST1: Not at all, and this is where the counterintuitive finding comes in. The importance distribution is wildly different across layers. At the mid-encoder, block eleven, the top feature causes a CRPS degradation of 38.61. The max-to-median ratio is over thirty times. A tiny handful of features are disproportionately critical, and they're the change-detection features. At the early encoder, you see a similar skewed pattern, max-to-median of about twenty-eight.
HOST2: And the final encoder? The one with all the rich semantic features?
HOST1: Here's the twist. The final encoder's importance distribution is nearly uniform. Max-to-median ratio of only about four in the base sweep. Each feature matters a little, but none of them matter a lot individually. And when they did progressive ablation, removing features one by one in order of importance, the results are even more dramatic.
HOST2: What happens?
HOST1: At the mid-encoder, progressive ablation is catastrophic. CRPS goes from 2.61 to 25.32 as you remove sixty-four features. The model basically falls apart. At the early encoder, similar story, big degradation. But at the final encoder? Removing features actually improves forecast quality. CRPS drops from 3.62 to 2.73. It gets better.
HOST2: Wait. Removing features from the richest, most semantically labeled layer makes the forecasts more accurate?
HOST1: Yep. And they confirmed it with an extended run. Two hundred features ablated with more samples and more windows. CRPS stays essentially flat, going from 3.59 to 3.58. So at best it's neutral, and in the standard sweep it's actually helpful.
HOST2: That's wild. What's the explanation?
HOST1: The authors suggest that the final encoder contains features that help Chronos generalize across its diverse pretraining domains, lots of different kinds of time series. But for the specific benchmark they tested on, ETT electricity transformer data, some of those general features actually add noise. Ablating them acts like implicit domain adaptation. You're stripping away the model's attempts to be general-purpose and leaving the features that actually help for this specific dataset.
HOST2: That's a really important finding for deployment. It suggests the model might be over-parameterized in its final representations. You could potentially prune the last layer and get better performance on specific domains.
HOST1: Right. And the broader takeaway is that causal importance and semantic richness are inversely related in this model. The layers that contain the most interpretable, taxonomically rich features are the least causally critical for actual performance. And the layer that really drives the forecast, the mid-encoder, is dominated by one conceptually simple but causally vital function: detecting abrupt changes.
HOST2: So Chronos isn't really a pattern matcher that learns beautiful seasonal cycles. It's more of a shock detector that cares most about regime changes.
HOST1: At least on this data, yes. And that should give pause to anyone who assumes that because a model captures complex patterns in its representations, those patterns must be driving the output. The causal story can be very different from the representational story.
HOST2: Limitations?
HOST1: Several. This is one model family, Chronos-T5-Large only. The taxonomy classifier is heuristic, based on correlations with synthetic data. Over eighty percent of features globally remain unlabeled. Decoder coverage is below six percent. And the ablation experiments only used one dataset, ETT. The paradoxical improvement at the final encoder might not replicate on other domains.
HOST2: Still, as a first application of SAEs to time series foundation models, this opens a lot of doors. If the methodology transfers, we could start really understanding what these models learn about temporal dynamics.
HOST1: And the headline finding, that the causally critical computation happens in the middle, not where you'd expect it at the end, is the kind of insight that only comes from actually opening the box. You can't get that from input-output analysis alone.
HOST2: Chronos's midlife crisis. The most important features are having their existential moment right in the middle of the network.
HOST1: Perfect way to put it.
