HOST1: Let's talk about sparse autoencoders, because they've become the go-to tool for mechanistic interpretability over the last couple of years. But there's a dirty secret that nobody likes to talk about.
HOST2: The junk drawer problem?
HOST1: The junk drawer problem. You train an SAE with, say, sixty-five thousand neurons, and you expect each one to light up for a clean, interpretable concept. Dog ears. Sarcasm. The color blue. But when you actually audit what these neurons do, the majority are either uninterpretable, meaning you can't figure out what concept they represent, or they're interpretable but not steerable, meaning they activate for a concept but intervening on them doesn't actually change the model's behavior.
HOST2: So you've got a dictionary with sixty-five thousand entries and most of them are either gibberish or decorative.
HOST1: That's a great way to put it. This paper from UC San Diego and Lawrence Livermore National Lab actually quantified it. They introduced new metrics for both interpretability and steerability at the neuron level and found that only about nineteen percent of SAE neurons score high on both. Nineteen percent! The other eighty-one percent are either hard to interpret, hard to steer, or both.
HOST2: That's brutal. So when people say "we used an SAE to understand the model," they're working with less than a fifth of the features?
HOST1: In terms of features that are both human-understandable and causally effective, yes. And there's a second problem that's almost worse. Because SAEs are unsupervised, they learn whatever structure happens to be in the data. But the concepts you actually care about might not show up. They found that twenty-seven to forty-five percent of concepts from standard benchmarks were simply absent from the SAE's dictionary, even when the SAE was trained on data that contained those concepts.
HOST2: So it's not just that most neurons are junk. It's that the concepts you went looking for might not be there at all.
HOST1: Right. Imagine you want to understand how a vision-language model represents "fluffy" or "metallic" or "crowded." You train your SAE, scan through sixty-five thousand neurons, and maybe a third of those concepts just don't have a corresponding feature. The SAE decided to use its capacity on other things.
HOST2: OK so what's the fix? You can't just keep making SAEs bigger and hoping the concepts show up.
HOST1: The fix is CB-SAE, Concept Bottleneck Sparse Autoencoders. And the insight is to combine two existing ideas that have complementary strengths. SAEs are great at unsupervised discovery, they find things you didn't know to look for. Concept Bottleneck Models are great at supervised concept alignment, they guarantee that specific concepts are represented. CB-SAE merges them.
HOST2: How does the merging actually work?
HOST1: It's a two-step process, and I think the elegance is in how practical it is. Step one: prune. You take your trained SAE and you evaluate every neuron on both interpretability and steerability using their new metrics. If a neuron scores low on either, you cut it. This gets rid of the dead weight, the junk drawer neurons that aren't helping anyone.
HOST2: So you're Marie Kondo-ing the SAE. Does this neuron spark joy? Does it spark interpretability AND steerability? No? Goodbye.
HOST1: Exactly. And step two: augment. After pruning, you've got gaps in your representation. You fill those gaps with a lightweight concept bottleneck. This is a small autoencoder that's explicitly trained to align with a user-defined concept set. Whatever concepts you care about, you specify them upfront, and the concept bottleneck guarantees they'll be represented.
HOST2: But wait, if I have to specify concepts upfront, doesn't that defeat the purpose of unsupervised discovery? I thought the whole point of SAEs was finding things you didn't know to look for.
HOST1: Great pushback, and the authors address this directly. The retained SAE neurons still handle unsupervised discovery. You're only pruning the low-quality ones. So you keep the SAE's ability to surface unexpected concepts, but you supplement it with guaranteed coverage of the concepts you know you need. It's unsupervised discovery plus supervised reliability.
HOST2: OK so how does the concept bottleneck part actually work? How do you align neurons to specific concepts?
HOST1: They use CLIP-Dissect, which is a technique that maps neurons to text concepts using a vision-language model. For each neuron, you find which text description best matches its activation pattern across a dataset of images. For the concept bottleneck, you define your concept set, say a hundred concepts like "furry," "wooden," "round," "outdoor," and you train the bottleneck autoencoder so that specific neurons correspond to specific concepts, with the alignment enforced through the training loss.
HOST2: And the training loss balances what exactly?
HOST1: Three things. Reconstruction fidelity, because you still need the autoencoder to faithfully reconstruct the original activations. Interpretability, because each concept neuron should cleanly correspond to its assigned concept. And steerability, because intervening on a concept neuron should actually change the model's behavior in the expected way. The loss function explicitly optimizes for all three.
HOST2: What do the numbers look like?
HOST1: Plus thirty-two percent improvement in interpretability and plus fourteen-and-a-half percent improvement in steerability across their experiments. They tested on two downstream applications. First, controlled text generation with large vision-language models, specifically LLaVA. And second, controlled image generation with UnCLIP. In both cases, CB-SAE substantially outperformed standard SAEs.
HOST2: What does steering look like in practice? Like give me a concrete example.
HOST1: So imagine you have an image of a cat, and you want the vision-language model to describe it as if the cat were in a snowy scene. With a standard SAE, you'd have to hunt through sixty-five thousand neurons hoping one of them corresponds to "snowy." Maybe you find one, maybe you don't. With CB-SAE, if "snowy" is in your concept set, there's a guaranteed neuron for it. You crank up that neuron's activation and the model starts describing snow in the scene. Same with image generation via UnCLIP. Amplify the "metallic" concept neuron and the generated image takes on metallic qualities.
HOST2: That's a much cleaner workflow than the current SAE treasure hunt.
HOST1: Right. And there's a subtlety in their pruning that I think is important. They don't just prune by interpretability alone. A neuron might light up cleanly for "dog" but intervening on it might not actually change the model's output. That's a neuron with high interpretability but low steerability. It's reading a signal but not writing one. CB-SAE prunes those too, because for practical applications, you need features that are both readable and writable.
HOST2: This reminds me of a distinction in neuroscience. Some brain regions are correlated with behaviors but not causal. You can observe them fire during a task, but lesioning them doesn't affect the task. Same thing here?
HOST1: Very similar analogy. And it's a problem the interpretability field has been slow to grapple with. A lot of SAE-based interpretability work stops at "we found a neuron that activates for this concept" without asking "does intervening on this neuron actually control the concept?" CB-SAE forces you to care about both.
HOST2: OK limitations. What are the catches?
HOST1: The obvious one is that you need to define your concept set upfront. If you don't know what you're looking for, if you're doing pure exploratory interpretability, vanilla SAEs still have a role. CB-SAE is best when you have specific concepts you want to represent and steer.
HOST2: Is there a computational overhead?
HOST1: The concept bottleneck itself is lightweight. It's much smaller than the full SAE. And the pruning step actually reduces the total number of active neurons, so in practice, the augmented model might not be much larger than the pruned SAE. The interpretability and steerability metrics they introduce are also designed to be computationally inexpensive, which matters when you're evaluating tens of thousands of neurons.
HOST2: Where does this sit in the broader SAE landscape? There's been a lot of SAE work lately. Anthropic's scaling laws, DeepMind's Gemma SAEs, the SAEBench benchmark.
HOST1: I think it sits in a really practical niche. A lot of the other work is about training better SAEs from scratch, improving the architecture or the training procedure. CB-SAE is a post-hoc framework. You can take any existing trained SAE and apply CB-SAE to it. Prune the junk, add the concepts you need. That's appealing because it doesn't require retraining from scratch.
HOST2: And there's a concurrent paper, AlignSAE, doing something similar?
HOST1: Yes, though with a different approach. AlignSAE tries to disentangle supervised concepts from unsupervised ones using an orthogonality loss, keeping everything in the same latent space. CB-SAE takes a more surgical approach, explicitly cutting out the low-quality neurons and grafting on new ones. And CB-SAE focuses on vision encoders and multimodal models, while AlignSAE targets text-based LLMs. So they're complementary.
HOST2: The bottom line is: if you're using SAEs for practical interpretability and steering, you should probably be pruning the junk and adding explicit concepts.
HOST1: That's the takeaway. SAEs are a powerful tool, but they have a junk drawer problem and a coverage problem. CB-SAE addresses both, with meaningful improvements in the metrics that actually matter for downstream use. It's not a revolution, but it's the kind of practical improvement that makes existing tools significantly more useful.
HOST2: The Marie Kondo of mechanistic interpretability.
HOST1: Does this SAE neuron spark interpretability and steerability? If not, thank it for its service and let it go.
