HOST1: Alright, so here's a question for you. You're building a multi-agent system, right? You've got your agents, your tools, your whole setup. You spend weeks picking the perfect model. GPT-5-mini, Gemini Flash, whatever. But here's the thing nobody tells you: your choice of framework might matter just as much as your choice of model.
HOST2: Wait, really? Like, whether I use LangGraph versus smolagents versus LlamaIndex could swing my results as much as swapping the entire language model?
HOST1: That's exactly what this paper found. And honestly, it kind of makes sense once you think about it, but nobody had actually measured it before. This is MASEval, from Parameter Lab, Oxford, Tubingen, NAVER AI Lab, and KAIST. And their whole argument is: we've been benchmarking wrong.
HOST2: OK so what do you mean benchmarking wrong? Like, GAIA and AgentBench are just... not good enough?
HOST1: Not quite. Those benchmarks are fine at what they do. The problem is what they don't do. When you read a benchmark paper and it says GPT-4 achieves 85 percent on task X, that number conflates two completely different things. It mixes up the model's capability with the framework's implementation. The orchestration logic, the error handling, how tool calls get formatted, all of that stuff.
HOST2: So basically, when someone reports a score, we don't actually know how much of that score comes from the model being smart versus the framework doing a good job of wiring things together.
HOST1: Exactly. And for single-agent setups, maybe that was OK. You pick a framework, you hold it constant, you compare models, fine. But with multi-agent systems, the framework choices multiply. You've got communication protocols between agents, memory architectures, role differentiation, how agents take turns, how errors propagate. All of these are framework decisions, not model decisions.
HOST2: OK so MASEval tries to fix this. What does it actually look like in practice?
HOST1: So it's not a benchmark itself. It's evaluation infrastructure. Think of it like a test harness that sits between your agent framework and your benchmark. The key idea is what they call "Bring Your Own." You bring your own framework, your own model, your own logging, and MASEval provides the orchestration, tracing, and lifecycle management around it.
HOST2: So it's kind of like PyTorch Lightning for agent evaluation? You write the interesting parts, it handles the boilerplate?
HOST1: That's a great analogy, and actually one they use in the paper. They have thin adapters for each framework. So for smolagents, which uses code-based tool calling and a pretty minimalist design, they have one adapter. For LangGraph, which is all about explicit state machines and state management, they have another. LlamaIndex, another one. And the core of MASEval never imports any framework-specific code. They actually enforce this in their CI pipeline.
HOST2: That's a nice design decision. So what happens when you actually run an evaluation?
HOST1: Each task goes through five phases. Setup, Execute, Collect, Evaluate, Report. The important bit is that during execution, every agent maintains its own independent message history. So you get per-agent traces. If something goes wrong in a multi-agent conversation, you can actually see which agent said what, when, and trace back exactly where the coordination failed.
HOST2: OK the architecture sounds clean. But the real question is the experiments. What did they actually find?
HOST1: Right, so they ran a full factorial experiment. Three frameworks: smolagents, LangGraph, and LlamaIndex. Three models: GPT-5-mini, Gemini 3.0 Flash, and Claude Haiku 4.5. All roughly in the same price and latency tier. Three benchmarks covering different things: MACS for enterprise coordination tasks, ConVerse for security and adversarial robustness, and MultiAgentBench for collaboration and competition.
HOST2: And they held everything else constant? Same tool definitions, same agent topologies, same prompts?
HOST1: As much as possible, yes. They kept the agent architecture the same and varied only the framework and model. The only things that differed were the framework's built-in defaults: system prompt formatting, how tools get mounted, error handling behavior. Things the framework decides for you.
HOST2: OK so what were the actual numbers?
HOST1: So across all six task domains, the average performance range across models was 14.2 percentage points. And across frameworks? 12.4 percentage points. Those are remarkably close. The standard deviations tell the same story: 7.5 for models, 6.5 for frameworks.
HOST2: So the framework effect is like 87 percent the size of the model effect. That's... significant.
HOST1: Very significant. And in two of the six domains, the framework effect actually exceeded the model effect. But the really wild finding was in the interaction effects.
HOST2: What do you mean?
HOST1: So take Claude Haiku 4.5 on the MACS Travel task. With smolagents, it scores 90.4 percent. With LlamaIndex? 59.5 percent. That's a 31 percentage point gap, same model, different framework.
HOST2: 31 points! That's enormous. What's causing that?
HOST1: And here's where it gets even more interesting. They found this case where GPT-5-mini specifically struggles with smolagents. The framework forces a tool call at every single step. GPT-5-mini responds to this by obsessively asking the user clarifying questions. And when the benchmark caps those questions at five and returns an error, GPT-5-mini doesn't adjust its strategy. It just retries the same tool call up to 23 times, slightly rephrasing each time.
HOST2: So the model is getting stuck in a loop because of how the framework handles errors?
HOST1: Exactly. It burns ten times more tokens than other models on the same task. And no other model exhibits this failure with smolagents. And GPT-5-mini doesn't exhibit this failure with other frameworks. It's a framework-model interaction that neither component would produce in isolation.
HOST2: That's actually a really important finding. It means you can't just benchmark a model and assume it'll perform the same way in your production setup.
HOST1: Right. And this is the whole point of the paper. When someone asks which model should I use, the answer might genuinely depend on which framework you're using. And vice versa.
HOST2: OK so what about the practical side? They claim they reduce implementation effort?
HOST1: Yeah, they measured lines of code for reimplementing benchmarks in MASEval versus the original codebases. The orchestration boilerplate, the stuff that wires components together, shrinks by 83 to 91 percent. Total implementation effort drops 35 to 57 percent.
HOST2: That's pretty compelling if you're a benchmark author or someone who wants to run evaluations across multiple frameworks.
HOST1: And they also built in adaptive testing support using Item Response Theory. The idea is you don't need to run every single benchmark task. You can estimate overall performance from just 1 percent of tasks with roughly 2 percentage points of error. That matters when a single full benchmark run on frontier models can cost tens of thousands of dollars.
HOST2: OK so let's step back. What are the limitations here?
HOST1: A few things. First, they only tested three frameworks and three models within one price tier. The framework effect might be smaller or larger at different capability levels. They acknowledge that comparing across tiers, like GPT-5-mini against GPT-5-Pro, would likely show larger model effects.
HOST2: Which makes sense. If your model is way more capable, maybe it can compensate for bad framework choices.
HOST1: Possibly. They've also only tested with up to five agents. Tracing overhead grows linearly, but they haven't pushed it to really large multi-agent systems. And the Bring Your Own philosophy means higher initial setup cost. You need to write adapters. It's not plug and play.
HOST2: But the adapter contract is minimal, right? Just run the agent and grab its messages?
HOST1: Yeah, that's the tradeoff. More upfront work, but complete flexibility. No vendor lock-in, no cloud dependency.
HOST2: So bottom line. What should someone actually do with this information?
HOST1: Three things. If you're building multi-agent systems, don't just benchmark models in isolation. Test them in your actual framework. Second, if you're evaluating frameworks, keep the model constant and measure the framework's contribution independently. And third, watch out for interaction effects. The fact that a specific model works great with one framework doesn't mean it'll work great with another. Test the combination you're actually going to deploy.
HOST2: And I'd add: if you're publishing benchmark results, consider reporting the framework alongside the model. Without that, other people can't reproduce your numbers.
HOST1: Really good point. The era of reporting just the model name and a score might need to end. The system is the unit of analysis now.
