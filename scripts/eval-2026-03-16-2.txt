HOST1: Right, and that's exactly the problem this paper exposes. A static benchmark is what you're describing. A fixed dataset of questions and answers. M-GSM has the same math problems every time. Belebele has the same reading comprehension passages. MMLU has the same multiple choice questions. You run your model, get a score, put it on the leaderboard, and call it a day.

HOST2: And functional benchmarks are different how?

HOST1: A functional benchmark uses templates. Instead of a fixed question like "Sally bought two red apples and three green apples, how much fruit did she buy," you have a template where the name, the numbers, the colors are all variables. Each time you run the evaluation, you generate fresh instances from those templates. Fifty variants, a hundred variants. The math is the same structure, but the specific numbers change every time.

HOST2: So you're testing whether the model can actually do the reasoning, not just whether it memorized the answer to this specific problem.

HOST1: Exactly. And this team, led by Victor Ojewale at Brown, did something nobody had done systematically before. HOST1: Right. And the gap between static and functional tells you how much of your benchmark score is real and how much might be memorization or pattern matching.

HOST2: And that gap is twenty-four percent in English?

HOST1: For the math benchmarks, yes. Models scored twenty-four percent lower on CL-GSM Symbolic compared to the static M-GSM in English. Seventeen percent lower in French. Eighteen percent in Spanish. These are huge drops. If your model gets an eighty on M-GSM, its real functional performance might be closer to sixty.

HOST2: That's genuinely alarming. But hold on. Is this necessarily memorization? Maybe the functional variants are just harder somehow?

HOST1: That's a fair question. The templates are designed so that the difficulty should be comparable. You're not changing the mathematical structure, just the specific numbers and names.
